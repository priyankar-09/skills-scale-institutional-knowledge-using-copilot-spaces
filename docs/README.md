## Project Management Process Overview

OctoAcme employs a structured, transparent approach to project management, emphasizing clear roles, strong communication routines, and quality-focused workflows. The project lifecycle begins with Initiation—where a business need is validated through a one-pager outlining objectives, success metrics, stakeholders, and a high-level timeline. As projects transition into Planning, the team conducts kickoff meetings, creates a prioritized and estimated backlog, formalizes a Definition of Done, and maps out risks, dependencies, and release milestones. These processes are standardized with checklist-driven templates, ensuring consistent scope and delivery.

Core roles are clearly defined and include the Project Manager (coordination, risk, and communication), Product Manager (vision, requirements, and prioritization), Developers (implementation, testing, and documentation), and stakeholders who supply input and approvals. These personas are critical to maintaining accountability and supporting rapid onboarding, with each accountable for specific artifacts and communication loops.

Communication is intentionally structured: daily standups address progress and blockers, weekly syncs and reporting keep alignment and surface risks, and milestone-based demo/review sessions ensure stakeholders are informed and engaged. Templates for risk registers, status updates, and incident communications ensure that all key information is captured and easy to reference. Team culture places high value on psychological safety, iterative delivery, and feedback, supporting continuous improvement.

Quality assurance is embedded throughout the workflow, with requirements for unit, integration, and end-to-end tests, mandatory code reviews, and automated CI pipelines for linting and security. Critical flows undergo manual QA before release, and clear escalation procedures exist for blockers and incidents. After each sprint or release, formal retrospectives capture learnings and drive actionable improvements, closing the loop for true continuous improvement.
